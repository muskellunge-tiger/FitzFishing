Strawberry Report 2019

Strategies

Spring

The one shore fishing trip we made to Strawberry in the spring was right after ice off. While the fishing was a little slow there was quite a few methods that produced some nice Rainbows and Cutthroats. The main thing that you would want to look for was steep drop-offs near shore. The easiest way to do this is to look for where the shore line is steep or on top of or around cliffs. The first method we used was casting 2" white tube jigs with a 3/8oz. head tipped with Chub meat. The retrieve that we used was a jig and reel down method. For an in-depth tutorial on how to use this retrieve please read the casting section on tube jigs below. While this method was not the best numbers producer it did produce the biggest fish of the day, a 20" Cutthroat.

The second method that worked was casting large Lucky Craft minnowbaits near the dam on the Solider Creek side of Strawberry. The retrieve that we used was a stop and go retrieve, and most of the time the fish would hit right at shore after your last crank. The only time this method would work is when we would see small Chub swimming along the shore getting eaten by Cutthroats. This meant that you would want your lure to look as close to a Chub as possible. The other thing to watch would be how the Chub swam in the water, so you could as best you could replicate that motion to increase your bites. This is why we used the stop and go retrieve. If you didn't see Chub swimming around this method would most likely not work.

The final method that worked was casting large spoons like Kamloopers and Pot-O-Golds. The best colors to use where gold and silver. This method was best to use around cliffs on the Solider Creek side when fish where hitting the surface. When retrieving, you would want to just retrieve at a steady speed. This method seemed to be the best way to catch Rainbows, and the Pot-O-Golds did better than the Kamloopers.

Summer

Kokanee

In 2019 the Kokanee fishing at Strawberry was decent. When trolling for them we used pop gear and triangle flashers with a wedding ring or pink squid tipped with a pink maggot. When it came to how far you put the lures behind the pop gear or flashers for us it didn't really matter. Anything from 6" all the way to 3 feet seemed to work. The best pop gear to use was silver or silver and gold willow leaf or tomahawk bladed pop gear. The same colors worked with the flashers. The pop gear was better than the flashers. The hardest part about catching the Kokanee was finding what depth they were at, but once you found it you could catch quite a few of them.

Cutthroat

Because the Cutthroat population is so strong in Strawberry, there is quite a few ways to catch them throughout the year but in the summer, there are two methods that work the best: trolling and tube jigs. Both of these methods are a great way to catch a lot of Cutthroats in a day as long as you use the right gear and the right methods. Both of these methods are capable of catching slot-buster sized Cutthroats, and maybe the occasional Rainbow.

Trolling

When trolling for Cutthroats we find pop gear to be the best option, but triangle flashers and dodgers also work well. The best pop gear to use is silver willow leaf with half a worm anywhere from 6" to 3 feet behind the pop gear. The biggest part of trolling for Cutthroats at Strawberry is making sure that your gear is at the correct depth, and knowing what depth your pop gear runs at will really help you out. For example, if you are trolling at 1 MPH. with no weight and 100 feet of line out then your gear is around 25 feet down, but the fish are only really this deep in early summer and fall. The rest of the time they are deeper, around 40+ feet, so you will need to add some weight. Remember that it doesn't take much weight to get the gear deeper plus, the more weight you have the harder it's going to be to reel especially if you are using pop gear with a lot of blades. Now, if you have down riggers then you don't need to worry about adding weight, just simply put your down rigger to the depth the fish are coming through at.
