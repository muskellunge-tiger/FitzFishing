Strategies

Late Winter/Spring

When fishing Good Ski in the early part of the year, fishing on the bottom was the way to go. There were a few methods that you could use in order to these bottom dwellers. The first, and probably most effective was bottom bouncing either 1.5 to 2" orange or yellow tube jigs or 1" green and white Marabou's. The second would be pretty much bottom fishing with a jig. And the third would be fishing on the bottom with bait.

The most important thing to remember when bottom bouncing these tube jigs is that a lighter jig head is better. The reasons for this are that it's going to take longer for the jig to hit the bottom which gives the fish a little more time to see the jig and it may also attract suspended fish in the area as well. The other reason a lighter jig head is good is because you will have a much lower chance of snagging up and while the bottom at Good Ski is fairly clean, there is still a chance that you could snag up on something if you are using a heavy jig head. When it came to the bottom bouncing itself, working slow and being patient would be very important. After casting out as far as you can, and the jig hits the bottom. Wait around 10 seconds, the reason for this is because any fish that saw the jig falling might be checking it out, and quite often while youâ€™re waiting the fish will just pick it up. If this doesn't happen, reel up the slack and do a slow vertical jig reel down and wait for the jig to hit the bottom again. Once it has hit the bottom again wait a few second to see if a fish takes. If this doesn't happen repeat the process. Also remember that this is a finesse tactic so, if you are not used to this type of fishing you will miss a lot of fish, this also goes for people who are experienced with finesse fishing. What you have to remember is that this is how finesse fishing works. Now, you will likely get hit on most of your casts if you are in the right area, so you will start to get the hang of it fairly quick. But, the biggest thing to remember when doing this is, when you see a bite don't set the hook right away you will miss pretty much every time. Instead wait a few seconds for the fish to take most of the slack out of the line, and fully eat the jig before setting the hook.

Sometimes when you're bottom bouncing jigs you will begin to notice that you are getting hit in about the same spot on every cast, and sometimes this isn't just a coincidence. It can mean that there are a lot of fish stacked in that one area. So, if you suspect this might be the case what you'll want to do is cast your jig in to the general area that you have been getting hit at and just let the jig sink to the bottom, just like you would when bottom bouncing. However, this time you are just going to want to leave the jig sitting on the bottom in the spot that you casted. If you do happen to cast into an area that has a school of fish then it is likely that you will get hit before or right as the jig hits the bottom. However, if you don't get hit right away this doesn't mean that you are in the wrong area. It could just mean that the fish are not as aggressive and need to look at the jig before biting or you could have casted a little off where they were and you may need to give them time to come to it. The best thing to do if you don't get right away is to let the jig sit for a minute or two and if you still haven't gotten hit then bottom bounce your jig back instead of just reeling it back. This is because you still want to give yourself a chance to catch a fish. And remember, just like bottom bouncing, don't set the hook right away cause you will likely miss the fish. If you end up going multiple casts without getting a hit using this method then it is best for you to just go back to bottom bouncing because you will cover more water that way.

For both of these methods we would always tip our jigs with pink maggots, but if you wanted to use worms, corn, or whatever it would probably work as well. The reason for this is because a tube jig or Marabou by itself is usually not enough to get the fish to hit. So, by adding bait to the jig, it would give it some sent to draw the fish in better, and usually get them to bite better too.

When fishing on the bottom with bait, the best bait for us was half a worm and half a marshmallow on about a 6" leader, but you could probably go as long as 12" if you wanted. The way we would weight our bait to get it to the bottom was by using a standard sized water bubble. If you don't know what a water bubble is, please read the paragraph below.

If you ever ran into a situation where the tube jigs or bottom fishing weren't working at all. Then you could try to fish with spinners or a bobber. The spinner that did work for us was a Li'l green Jake, and when fishing with the bobber we used half a worm and put it about 2' below the bobber. However, while these methods would work, it seemed that they wouldn't work near as good as the tube jigs or bottom fishing. So, most of the time if those two methods didn't work we would just leave and try another day.

Fall

When fishing Good Ski in the fall we learned very quickly that it was going to be a miracle if caught a fish. Which was a little shocking due to how clear and cool the water was, and the fact that it had done so well last fall. The one time we caught fish we were fishing on the bottom with half a worm and half a marshmallow, but the only thing we caught were small Perch. We also tried spinners, spoons, and tube jigs with no luck at all on multiple trips.

What is a Water-bubble?

A water-bubble is a round oval like clear bubble that you fill with water to allow baits to sink to the bottom. Now the reason I use this over snap weights is because the water-bubble allows the line to slide through it, so the fish won't feel any tension when it takes your bait. This will cause the fish to hold and eat your bait for longer allowing you to get a better hook set. It will also allow you to get a longer cast over snap weights.
